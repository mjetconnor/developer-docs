Spigit in The Graph

The SpigitGraph data model is designed to cover a broad range of current and future social innovation applications.  The stand-out application in that space at this time is Spigit.  You are probably reading this doc right now with an eye towards creating your own apps that interact with Spigit instances.

It is important to remember that the SpigitGraph data model covers a broader use case than Spigit.  

Many of the concepts and specifics in The Graph are designed with an eye towards extensions and innovations that may come along later, either in future versions of Spigit or in entirely new application stacks.  This is what makes it possible for you to write applications that are not constantly upended by the latest Spigit additions.  We will continue to create industry-leading innovations in Spigit while your applications continue to reliably deliver their special sauce.

As you become more familiar with the Graph data model, you will find that some things are rigidly prescribed while other parts retain quite a bit of flexibility.  For instance, some properties are defined to require values of a certain class, but that class may be a super-class with some interesting child classes.  Where the Graph model speaks of one class, Spigit may commonly return data belonging to a different child class.  For example, properties in the Graph model are frequently declared to be of type Agent, whereas these are almost always Users, a kind of Agent, when coming from Spigit.  Similarly, where the Graph model talks about Inquiries, these are usually actually Challenges (sometimes SuggestionBoxes) when coming from Spigit.

Also, take note of the "Req" column in the class definition topics.  Many properties are defined as MAY or [ * ], which means that it is legal for the property to have a value or not have a value (or have multiple values in the later case), depending on context such as the details of the Spigit instance your app is interacting with.  In the section that details Spigit behavior, the rules may be more specific for certain class properties.  Some will be known to always have values of a certain type, others may be known to never be returned by Spigit.  No matter what though, data returned from Spigit will never contradict the rules declared by SpigitGraph.

And, it is always legal for an entity to have more properties than the documented ones.  That just means that those properties are, well, undocumented.  Just be aware that if your application uses those properties, it may not be compatible with future versions of Spigit or future non-Engage applications.
